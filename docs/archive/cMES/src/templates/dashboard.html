🏭 MES Recommendations — Ranked by Impact

  • Priority 1 — Add a 2nd filling machine: Reduces mean flow time by ~47% (703 → 373 min). Consistent with M/M/2 queueing prediction (Wq 120 → 8 min, −93%). Single largest improvement possible.
  • Priority 2 — Standardise procedures (reduce CV 50%): Reduces flow time by ~11% (703 → 628 min). Achievable through tighter SOPs, operator training, and automated dispensing.
  • Priority 3 — Implement SPT dispatching at filling: Statistically significant 7-min reduction (p = 0.04, CRN paired t-test). Zero capital cost — a software change in the MES.
  • EDD dispatching: Not significantly different from FIFO in this model (p > 0.05). May still be preferred operationally when order lateness penalties are high.

Pharma MES — System Guide

ISE 573 Week 7  ·  Connor Sheridan  ·  University of Rhode Island
A Manufacturing Execution System prototype for a sterile injectable pharmaceutical facility. This guide explains every page, control, and metric in plain language.

What is this system?

This is a Manufacturing Execution System (MES) prototype that simulates how batches of sterile injectable medicine move through a pharmaceutical factory — from raw materials all the way to finished, approved product.

It demonstrates five engineering concepts in one integrated system:

1 — Queueing Theory
Mathematical prediction of how waiting lines form at each station (M/M/1, M/M/c, M/G/1 models)
2 & 3 — Simulation & Statistics
SimPy discrete-event simulation, 15 replications, Welch warm-up, 95% confidence intervals, paired t-test
4 — What-If Analysis
Testing 5 scenarios (e.g. adding a 2nd filling machine, changing dispatch rules) before committing to real change
5 — MES Integration
PostgreSQL database, MRP explosion, EDD/SPT scheduling, PuLP optimisation, ISA-95 Level 3 data flow
The Two Types of Simulation

There are two completely separate simulation engines in this project. Understanding the difference is the most important thing:

🟢 Live Dashboard Simulation
  • Runs while the browser is open
  • Watch individual batches (coloured dots) move in real time
  • Controlled by ▶ Start / ⏸ Pause / ↺ Reset
  • Used for visualisation and testing dispatch rules live
  • Not used for statistical conclusions
📊 Statistical Simulation
  • Run once beforehand via des_simulation.py
  • 15 complete factory replications × 5 scenarios
  • Each replication = ~10 simulated days
  • Takes ~3 minutes; produces the 6 charts in outputs/
  • Used for statistically reliable conclusions and CI

Analogy: The statistical simulation is like running a lab experiment 15 times and averaging the results. The live simulation is like a fishbowl you can watch.

The Factory Process — 9 Stations

Every batch of injectable drug goes through these stations in order:

StepStationWhat happensTime
①Raw Material StagingIncoming materials are logged and held~30 min
②Quarantine HoldMaterials tested and held pending results120 min (fixed)
③Dispensing & WeighingAPIs and excipients weighed out precisely~45 min
④Compounding / Solution PrepDrug dissolved and mixed with WFI water~90 min
⑤Sterile FiltrationSolution passed through 0.22 µm filter~60 min
⑥⚡ Aseptic FillingSolution filled into vials in cleanroom — BOTTLENECK~120 min
⑦Inspection & PackagingEach vial visually inspected; cartons assembled~45 min
⑧QA Release HoldQuality team reviews completed batch record180 min (fixed)
⑨Released ✓Batch approved for shipment—

Why is Filling the bottleneck? It has the longest processing time (120 min) relative to batch arrival rate (every 240 min avg). Utilisation = 50% — higher than every other station — so it builds up a queue first. Adding a 2nd filling machine cuts queue wait by 93% (M/M/2 analysis).

Dispatch Rules

When multiple batches are waiting for a machine, the system needs a rule to decide which goes next:

FIFO — First In, First Out
Oldest waiting batch goes first. Simplest and fairest — no batch ever starves.
SPT — Shortest Processing Time
Fastest-to-process batch goes first. Minimises average wait time. Our analysis found SPT gives ~7 min shorter flow time than FIFO (p = 0.04).
EDD — Earliest Due Date
Most urgent batch (closest deadline) goes first. Best for avoiding late orders — used in the MRP schedule.
